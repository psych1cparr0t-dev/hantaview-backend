Epidemiological context: Hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) and hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) together account for an estimated 150,000–200,000 hospitalizations annually worldwide (WHO, 2023). Case fatality ratios (CFR — the proportion of confirmed cases that result in death) vary markedly by species: Sin Nombre virus (SNV) ~36%; Andes virus (ANDV) ~25–35%; Puumala virus (PUUV) <1%; Hantaan virus (HTNV) 1–15%. No licensed vaccine exists for any hantavirus species outside of limited East Asian markets.

Aetiology & Natural HistoryWhat causes hantavirus disease and how it persists in animal populations
Hantaviruses (family Hantaviridae) are zoonotic (of animal origin — naturally maintained in non-human hosts; humans are incidental dead-end hosts) RNA viruses maintained in wild rodent reservoirs. Each species co-evolved with a specific host: Sin Nombre orthohantavirus (SNV) — Peromyscus maniculatus (deer mouse), western North America; Andes orthohantavirus (ANDV) — Oligoryzomys longicaudatus, Andean/Patagonian South America; Seoul orthohantavirus (SEOV) — Rattus norvegicus (Norway rat), cosmopolitan distribution; Puumala orthohantavirus (PUUV) — Myodes glareolus (bank vole), northern Europe.

Reservoir hosts sustain persistent, largely asymptomatic infection. Viral shedding (continuous release of infectious virus particles into bodily secretions by the reservoir host) occurs in urine, feces, and saliva. Human infection is a spillover event — not part of the natural transmission cycle. Estimated basic reproduction number (R₀) (R₀ — average number of new infections caused by one case in a fully susceptible population; values below 1.0 mean chains die out naturally) for ANDV in documented person-to-person clusters: 1.1–1.2, insufficient to sustain epidemic chains without continuous zoonotic amplification.
Routes of TransmissionHow the virus moves from animal reservoirs into humans
1
Inhalation of infectious aerosols — Primary route. Disturbance of dried rodent excreta in enclosed, poorly ventilated spaces generates respirable particles (<5 µm) carrying viable virus
2
Direct mucosal or percutaneous contact — Handling infected rodents or contaminated materials without barrier protection
3
Ingestion — Consumption of contaminated food or water; considered a minor route
4
Human-to-human (ANDV only) — Documented exclusively for Andes virus; requires close, prolonged contact with infectious secretions. Not observed for any other hantavirus species
Transmission not documented by: casual contact, airborne spread in normal ventilation conditions, fomites (inanimate objects or surfaces that carry infectious agents), arthropod vectors, or consumption of cooked food. Hantavirus does not possess the receptor-binding or environmental stability characteristics associated with airborne pathogens (e.g., SARS-CoV-2, M. tuberculosis).
Clinical Presentation (HPS/HCPS)How the illness progresses, stage by stage
Phase 1 — Prodromal (days 1–5) — early warning stage before the defining illness appears
Fever (38–40°C), myalgia (muscle pain), headache, malaise, nausea/vomiting. Thrombocytopaenia (low platelet count — impairs normal blood clotting) and elevated haematocrit (rising red blood cell fraction, a sign that fluid is leaking out of blood vessels) may be detected. Clinically indistinguishable from influenza-like illness.
Phase 2 — Cardiopulmonary (days 5–10) — critical stage of lung and circulatory failure
Rapid onset non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema (fluid flooding the lungs due to vessel leakage — not caused by heart failure); hypoxia (dangerously low blood-oxygen levels) requiring supplemental oxygen or mechanical ventilation; cardiogenic shock in severe cases. Radiograph: bilateral interstitial infiltrates (shadowing across both lungs on chest X-ray, indicating fluid infiltration). Marked thrombocytopaenia, elevated LDH (lactate dehydrogenase — a blood enzyme that rises sharply with widespread cell damage), haemoconcentration (thickening of the blood as plasma leaks into tissues).
Phase 3 — Convalescent — recovery stage
Rapid diuresis (increased urine output as accumulated fluid is cleared from the body) and clinical improvement in survivors. Full recovery may take weeks to months; persistent fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance reported. No chronic carrier state (long-term asymptomatic infection capable of transmitting to others) documented.
Management: No approved specific antiviral therapy for HPS. Treatment is supportive — early ICU admission, lung-protective ventilation (low-volume, low-pressure ventilator settings that minimise further injury to already inflamed lung tissue), vasopressors (drugs that constrict blood vessels to raise dangerously low blood pressure) for haemodynamic support, judicious fluid management. Ribavirin (a broad-spectrum antiviral that blocks viral RNA replication) has demonstrated in-vitro activity against multiple hantavirus species; clinical benefit in established HPS remains unproven. Extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) (a machine that temporarily takes over the work of the lungs and heart when they can no longer sustain life) has been used in severe refractory cases.

Clinical Decision PointWhen and how to seek medical evaluation after a possible exposure
Seek urgent medical assessment if fever, myalgia, or dyspnoea (difficulty breathing) develops within 45 days of potential rodent exposure or contact with a confirmed hantavirus case. Inform the treating clinician of the exposure history — early recognition is critical, as respiratory deterioration can be precipitous (abrupt and severe — patients can decline from mild symptoms to respiratory failure within hours). There is no reliable point-of-care test (a rapid bedside test — hantavirus requires specialist laboratory analysis); diagnosis requires PCR (polymerase chain reaction — detects viral genetic material in blood) or serological (blood antibody testing) confirmation at a reference laboratory.
